The Founding

Two Ladies, One Dream

Two Ladies Kitchen was founded in 1996 by Aunty Nora Uchida and her aunt Tomi Tokeshi in downtown Hilo on Hawaiʻi's Big Island. At the time, Nora was a full-time elementary school teacher — with no professional culinary background, but a lifelong love of mochi.

She spent nearly two years visiting Tomi's kitchen every Saturday, learning the craft entirely by hand — rolling, shaping, dusting — and giving away everything they made until friends and neighbors urged her to open a shop. Tomi herself had learned from a Buddhist priest trained in Japan, grounding the operation in a lineage of authentic tradition.

Nora had hoped to name it "Three Ladies Kitchen" — to honor her mother Sachi Kishimoto, who was also part of production — but Sachi quietly declined the spotlight. And so, Two Ladies Kitchen was born.

"Mochi is symbolic of good luck, and rice is nourishment for the deceased before they ascend to nirvana."
— Aunty Nora Uchida, Founder

The shop launched with seven mochi flavors and three manju varieties in a small commercial kitchen. Within a year, growing demand prompted a move to its current location on Kīlauea Avenue — doubling kitchen space and bringing the shop to street-front visibility in the heart of Hilo.

Carrying the Legacy Forward

New Hands, Same Heart

In 2019, Aunty Nora retired — and made a decision that spoke volumes about her character. She offered the business not to the highest bidder, but to her two most trusted longtime employees: Emerald Tuares and Clyde Ebanez.

Under Aunty Nora, the standard was uncompromising. During training, anything that fell short of perfect earned a quiet head shake — "No, not acceptable." Emerald and Clyde absorbed that ethos completely, and carry it into every single piece they make today.

"She offered it to us because she trusted us. She trusted that we would keep it the same."
— Emerald Tuares, Co-owner
"Make mochi with love... anything that you make, make it with love — cause it's gonna taste so much more amazing."
— Emerald Tuares, Co-owner

Know Your Ingredients

The Building Blocks of Mochi

Japanese confections use a handful of traditional fillings — here's what goes into ours.

Tsubushi An
Lumpy, sweet red azuki bean paste — a rustic, hearty filling with visible bean pieces and a rich, earthy sweetness.
Koshi An
Smooth, velvety azuki bean paste — silky and refined, with a clean, delicate sweetness.
Shiro An
White, smooth lima bean paste — mild and subtly sweet, it lets the mochi's outer flavors shine.
Kinako
Roasted soybean powder — nutty, toasty, and slightly sweet. Used as a coating on traditional mochi like Nantu and Kamato.
